# **The Global AI Race: Strategic Dynamics, Game Theoretic Outcomes, and the Geopolitical Crucible of 2025**

The pursuit of Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) has fundamentally transcended the boundaries of technological innovation to become the central organizing principle of global geopolitics and industrial strategy in the mid-2020s. By 2025, the "AI race" is no longer a rhetorical device used by Silicon Valley enthusiasts but a high-stakes "existential gamble" involving the total mobilization of national resources, the weaponization of semiconductor supply chains, and the fundamental restructuring of the global software economy.1 This report provides an exhaustive analysis of these dynamics, utilizing game theory to model the likely outcomes of the current competitive environment while synthesizing the impact of contemporary conflicts, capital reallocation trends, and the quest for compute dominance.

## **I. Defining the AI Race: Paradigms of Power and Cognitive Dominance**

The AI race in 2025 is defined by a frantic contest between corporate titans and nation-states to achieve a "one-time" technological threshold: the creation of a system that surpasses human cognitive power across all economically and strategically valuable domains.1 This race is characterized by two distinct paradigms of success, primarily championed by the United States and the People's Republic of China, which prioritize different end-states for artificial intelligence.

### **1\. The US Paradigm: AGI and Model Dominance**

For the United States and its leading AI laboratories—OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, and Meta—the race is defined as the pursuit of Artificial General Intelligence.1 This paradigm views AGI as a self-improving system capable of executing complex real-world knowledge work tasks better than humans.1 Success is measured by "model performance," with 2025 representing a pivotal year where "emerging" AGI systems like GPT-4 and its successors began demonstrating PhD-level intelligence in specific benchmarks.3 The American strategy relies on a massive "wagering" of capital on hundreds of billions of dollars in compute, hyperscale superclusters, and increasingly large language models, aiming for a "winner-take-all" advantage that triggers explosive economic growth.1

### **2\. The Chinese Paradigm: Embodied Intelligence and Industrial Transformation**

Beijing has adopted a more application-oriented definition, focusing on "embodied intelligence" and the "comprehensive intelligent transformation" of the physical economy.1 Rather than focusing solely on large language models—which some Chinese state officials have occasionally characterized as "capitalist drivel"—the 14th Five-Year Plan directs AI toward manufacturing, industrial production, and logistics.1 China's Ministry of Industry and Information Technology estimates that by the end of 2025, over 60 percent of large Chinese manufacturers will have integrated "AI \+ Manufacturing" systems.1 Their long-term objective is a 100 percent saturation of AI across key industrial sectors by 2035, leveraging their leadership in industrial robotics and clean energy to power a physical-world intelligence advantage.1

### **3\. Hierarchical Classification of Intelligence**

The classification of progress in this race has been formalized by researchers at Google DeepMind, who in 2023 proposed a five-level framework for AGI performance. This framework is crucial for understanding the current state of the race and the milestones being contested by global powers.6

| Performance Level | Definition | 2025 Status |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Level 1: Emerging** | Outperforms unskilled humans (e.g., ChatGPT, LLaMA 2). | Current baseline for all major models. |
| **Level 2: Competent** | Outperforms 50% of skilled adults in non-physical tasks. | Achieved by frontier models in coding and legal tasks. |
| **Level 3: Expert** | Outperforms 90% of skilled adults. | Target for 2025–2026 model releases. |
| **Level 4: Virtuoso** | Outperforms 99% of skilled adults. | Projected for late 2020s. |
| **Level 5: Superhuman** | Outperforms 100% of humans (Artificial Superintelligence). | The ultimate "finish line" of the race. |

This hierarchy clarifies that the AI race is not a singular event but a continuous cycle of improvement and refinement.6 However, the strategic perception remains that the actor who first achieves Level 5—Superhuman AGI—will possess an unassailable advantage in cyberwarfare, scientific discovery, and economic planning.1

## **II. Game Theory and the Logic of Escalation**

Game theory provides the most robust framework for predicting the outcomes of the AI race, as it formalizes the strategic interactions between agents whose outcomes depend on the choices of others.9 The current race is widely modeled as a "Prisoner’s Dilemma" (PD), although elements of the "Stag Hunt" and the "Game of Chicken" are increasingly visible in the 2025 landscape.4

### **1\. The Prisoner’s Dilemma: The Dominant Strategy of Defection**

In the AI race, the "Prisoner’s Dilemma" describes a scenario where two primary actors (e.g., the US and China, or OpenAI and Google) must choose between "Cooperation" (prioritizing safety, slowing down scaling, and sharing research) and "Defection" (racing ahead as fast as possible, cutting safety corners).4

The fundamental tragedy of the Prisoner’s Dilemma in this context is that although mutual cooperation would benefit everyone by reducing existential risk and ensuring safe alignment, the "rational" choice for each individual actor is to defect.4 Because reaching AGI is viewed as a "one-time threshold" that grants the winner a definitive advantage, there is no "iterated" game to enforce cooperation through future punishment.4

|  | Actor B: Cooperates (Safety) | Actor B: Defects (Race) |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Actor A: Cooperates (Safety)** | (3, 3\) Moderate, safe growth for both. | (0, 5\) Actor A is left behind; Actor B wins big. |
| **Actor A: Defects (Race)** | (5, 0\) Actor A wins big; Actor B is left behind. | (1, 1\) High risk of catastrophe; winner uncertain. |

The numerical payoffs illustrate that "racing" is the strictly dominant strategy.4 If Actor B cooperates, Actor A gets 5 by racing vs. 3 by slowing down. If Actor B races, Actor A gets 1 by racing vs. 0 by slowing down. Consequently, the logic of the matrix dictates that all players will race, leading to an equilibrium of "Mutual Defection".4 In 2025, this is manifested in the "AI safety is dead" narrative, where corporations and nations prioritize innovation velocity over ethical guardrails to avoid individual defeat.4

### **2\. The Stag Hunt and the Problem of Trust**

The "Stag Hunt" or "Assurance Game" presents a more optimistic but fragile alternative.10 In this model, players must decide whether to hunt a "Stag" (cooperate for a high-value, safe AGI) or a "Hare" (act individually for a smaller, guaranteed reward).10 The highest payoff (10, 10\) occurs if both cooperate. However, if one player fears the other will defect to catch a hare (7, 0), they will likely defect as well to avoid being left with nothing.10

In the AI race, "hunting the stag" represents global cooperation on safety standards and verification, while "hunting the hare" represents nationalistic "Sovereign AI" initiatives.10 The 2026 AI Impact Summit in India reflects an attempt to shift the game toward a Stag Hunt by emphasizing inclusive collaboration, yet the persistent "electron gap" and semiconductor export controls maintain a high degree of mutual suspicion.7

### **3\. Brinkmanship and the Game of Chicken**

As the race nears the AGI threshold, the dynamic begins to resemble the "Game of Chicken" (or Hawk-Dove).11 In this model, two actors drive toward each other; the one who "swerves" (slows down for safety) loses face, but if neither swerves, both perish in a catastrophic collision.11 By 2025, the proliferation of autonomous weapons and AI-enabled cyberwarfare has introduced "flash war" risks, where automated retaliation could escalate minor accidents into global conflicts before human intervention is possible.17 The refusal of the US and UK to sign the 2025 Paris Summit statement on sustainable AI highlights a "no-swerve" posture, as leaders fear that any unilateral slowdown will be exploited by adversaries.19

## **III. The SaaS Apocalypse: Capital Reallocation and Market Divestment**

The AI race has triggered a fundamental restructuring of the technology sector, particularly within the Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) ecosystem.20 By 2025, the traditional SaaS model, which relied on "per-seat" pricing and human-centered workflows, has encountered what analysts call the "AI Funding Apocalypse".20

### **1\. The Strategic Pivot: AI-Native vs. AI-Enhanced**

Venture capital investors in 2025 have radically shifted their focus away from "traditional" SaaS.20 AI startups received 53% of all global venture capital dollars in the first half of 2025, a concentration that has left traditional software founders "shut out" of the market.20 Investors are increasingly abandoning several categories of AI software that were popular only two years prior.23

| Categorical Shift | Abandoned (2023–2024) | Prioritized (2025–2026) |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Architecture** | "Bolt-on" AI features. | AI-native from the ground up. |
| **Pricing** | Rigid per-seat subscription. | Usage-based / Consumption-based. |
| **Value Prop** | Human coordination/workflow. | Autonomous agent execution. |
| **Integrations** | Specialized "connectors." | Universal protocols (e.g., Anthropic MCP). |
| **Moat** | Code complexity. | Proprietary data and workflow ownership. |

### **2\. Divestment and "Agentic" Acquisitions**

To survive this transition, major SaaS incumbents are divesting non-core business units to fund massive AI infrastructure builds and "Agentic" AI strategies.24 This is a defensive maneuver to hedge against "disruption risk" from agile, AI-native entrants.26

Salesforce has been the most active strategic buyer in 2025, completing 10 acquisitions—including the $8 billion cash offer for Informatica—to accelerate its "Agentforce" strategy.25 This strategy focuses on building autonomous digital workers that can manage entire customer service and sales workflows without human oversight.25 Similarly, ServiceNow has acquired niche technologies like Cuein and Data.World to enhance its AI-driven workflow automation platforms.25

Other notable 2025 divestments and acquisitions include:

* **Valsoft:** Acquired Quorum Information Technologies and KidKare to expand its portfolio of "mission-critical" vertical SaaS.25  
* **Visma:** Acquired milia.io and Taxy.io, two AI-driven platforms focusing on automating accounting and tax advisory workflows.25  
* **Volaris Group:** Acquired Surveypal and Maze Feedback to bolster its customer experience software with AI-driven analytics.25

### **3\. The Collapse of "Thin Wrappers"**

The 2025 market has been particularly brutal for startups building "thin AI wrappers" on existing models.23 As AI agents become capable of executing tasks directly, the value of tools designed solely for human coordination has plummeted.23 Technological advancements like Anthropic’s Model Context Protocol (MCP) have further simplified connections between AI models and external data, turning what were once valuable integration "moats" into basic utilities.23 Consequently, investors now prioritize "proprietary data moats" and companies that demonstrate clear "workflow ownership".20

## **IV. Geopolitical Crucible: War and the Battlefield Testing of AI**

The AI race is inextricably linked to the active conflicts in Ukraine and the Middle East, which have become the primary "test ranges" for next-generation autonomous systems.27 These wars are not just humanitarian crises but kinetic milestones where AI models are trained on real-time combat data, accelerating the development of lethal autonomous weapons (LAWS).27

### **1\. Ukraine: The Laboratory of Autonomous Warfare**

The Russia-Ukraine conflict has demonstrated the decisive role of AI in overcoming electronic warfare.28 Extensive Russian jamming has rendered GPS-guided precision munitions ineffective, prompting Ukraine and its Western allies to develop drones that use edge-AI for autonomous navigation and target recognition.28

Strategic developments in this theater include:

* **Autonomous Swarms:** The transition from tele-operated drones to autonomous swarms that can coordinate attacks without a direct human link.17  
* **Predictive Logistics:** The use of AI to analyze satellite imagery and sensor data to predict adversary movements and optimize the delivery of critical aid.27  
* **Manpower Reduction:** AI-driven robots are being used to venture into high-risk areas for medical evacuation and demining, reducing the manpower burden in a war of attrition.28

### **2\. The Middle East: Supply Chain Fragility and Energy Volatility**

The 2025 escalation in the Middle East has highlighted a critical vulnerability in the global AI race: the reliance on regional energy and specialized materials.14

| Impact Vector | Mechanism | Strategic Consequence |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Helium Supply** | Middle East is a primary source. | Bottleneck in semiconductor fabrication cooling. |
| **Energy Prices** | Volatility in Strait of Hormuz. | Spiking data center operating costs ($/TWh). |
| **Physical Infrastructure** | Drone strikes on data centers. | Delays in regional AI hub expansions (e.g., Amazon, Nvidia). |
| **Sovereign Cloud** | National security imperatives. | Acceleration of locally-owned, redundant infrastructure. |

South Korean chipmakers warned in early 2026 that conflict in the Middle East could disrupt the supply of helium gas, for which there is "no viable alternative" in the semiconductor production process.30 This represents a "high-severity shock" that could elevate DRAM and NAND pricing, directly increasing the infrastructure costs for AI deployments globally.14

### **3\. The Weaponization of Interdependence**

Semiconductors have become the "new weapon of geopolitics".32 The US "Small Yard, High Fence" strategy has effectively weaponized the semiconductor technology stack, restricting China's access to the most advanced GPUs (like the Nvidia H20 and Blackwell chips) required for AGI training.1 In retaliation, China has consolidated control over the "inputs" of the future—critical minerals like gallium, germanium, graphite, and tungsten—which are essential for the next generation of American chips.1 This "weaponized interdependence" creates a feedback loop where each actor’s attempt to secure their supply chain further destabilizes the other’s, reinforcing the Prisoner’s Dilemma equilibrium.1

## **V. The Compute War: Export Controls and the "Electron Gap"**

The AI race is fundamentally a "race for compute".3 In 2025, compute dominance is viewed as the primary indicator of national power, with 60–95% of recent AI performance gains attributed to scaling compute and data rather than algorithmic breakthroughs.3

### **1\. US Export Controls and the Compute Advantage**

As of March 2025, the United States controlled 75% of worldwide AI compute capacity, while China’s share had decreased to 14.1%.3 This disparity is a direct result of US export controls, which have evolved through the "AI Diffusion Rule" of early 2025 to group nations into tiers based on their alignment with US security interests.3

Key metrics of the 2025 compute war:

* **Training Scaling:** Compute requirements for frontier models have doubled every six months since 2015\.3  
* **Inference Scaling:** New "long thinking" (test-time scaling) techniques for GPT-5 class models can consume over 100x more compute than traditional inference.3  
* **Lithography Constraints:** Export controls continue to block China's access to ASML’s Extreme Ultraviolet (EUV) lithography machines.3 However, Chinese firms like SMIC have reportedly "cracked" 5nm production using complex Self-Aligned Quadruple Patterning (SAQP) with older Deep Ultraviolet (DUV) tools.3

### **2\. The "Electron Gap": China’s Energy Counter-Strategy**

While the US leads in high-end silicon, China possesses a significant advantage in the energy required to power those chips.1 The "Electron Gap" refers to China’s ability to build and scale data center energy infrastructure faster and more sustainably than the US.7

| Energy Metric (2030 Projections) | United States | China |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Data Center Electricity Demand** | 426 TWh (9% of total demand) | 277 TWh |
| **Clean Energy Leadership** | Emerging (Investment in Small Modular Reactors) | Global Leader (Solar, Wind, Batteries) |
| **Grid Constraints** | High (Regulatory hurdles for new projects) | Low (State-led infrastructure build) |
| **Strategic Advantage** | Access to most efficient GPUs. | Access to massive, cheap energy. |

The International Energy Agency forecasts that US data center electricity demand will more than double by 2030\.7 Unlike the US, where energy projects are often delayed by regulatory "patchworks," China's historically rapid pace of energy expansion ensures that energy supply is unlikely to be a constraint for their AI ambitions.7 David Victor of Brookings notes that while the US controls the "chips of today," China’s control over renewable energy and raw materials makes it harder for the US to build the "chips of tomorrow".7

## **VI. International Governance: Signaling, Summits, and the Failure of Verification**

The rapid advancement of AI has prompted a series of global summits aimed at establishing "red lines" for unacceptable risks, such as AI-enabled bioterrorism or unauthorized nuclear escalation.15 However, by 2026, these efforts are increasingly seen as "summitology as a dying art".15

### **1\. The Signaling Game of Middle Powers**

Middle powers like India, Brazil, and South Africa are using AI summits to signal their "strategic autonomy".15 India’s 2026 AI Impact Summit represents a pivotal shift away from "frontier competition" toward "absorption capacity"—the ability to translate AI into economic development for the Global Majority.16 Prime Minister Modi’s vision of "democratizing AI" positions India as a third pole that can "weather US and Chinese AI dominance".15

### **2\. The Verifiability Crisis**

The primary obstacle to a meaningful international AI treaty is the "verifiability" problem.35 Unlike nuclear non-proliferation, where inspectors can count warheads or detect uranium enrichment, AI progress is largely invisible.35

Current proposed verification mechanisms include:

* **Hardware Verification:** Installing "narrow-purpose verification hardware" inside data centers to monitor compute usage.35  
* **Mutually Verified Data Centers:** Creating international compute hubs that run "privacy-preserving computations" to allow for oversight without revealing proprietary model weights.35  
* **Technical Standards:** Building agreement on "red lines" and digital watermarks for deepfakes.15

Despite these proposals, the 2025 Paris Summit failed to produce substantial commitments to AI safety, as the US and UK refused to sign declarations that they felt would hamper their competitive velocity.19 The UN Scientific Panel on AI remains a hopeful "depoliticizing" venue, but its influence is currently limited to "soft law" and non-binding recommendations.15

### **3\. The Split in Global Norms**

A "patchwork" of conflicting regulations is emerging.39 The European Union’s AI Act remains the most comprehensive regulatory framework, focusing on managed risk ex-ante.16 Meanwhile, the US has favored a "light-touch" approach of voluntary commitments and executive orders (e.g., EO 14179\) to avoid stifling innovation.16 China combines rapid deployment with "strong political oversight," mandating that AI systems align with state priorities.16 This "diverging governance pathway" reinforces the game-theoretic perception that the AI race is a zero-sum contest where "regulatory drag" is a strategic liability.4

## **VII. Conclusion: Synthesis of Likely Outcomes and Strategic Recommendations**

The convergence of game theoretic pressures, SaaS divestments, and real-world military testing suggests that the AI race is entering its most volatile and decisive phase. Based on the analysis of current trends and the underlying strategic logic, several likely outcomes emerge for the 2026–2030 period.

### **1\. Likely Outcomes of the Race**

* **Bifurcation of the AI Ecosystem:** The world will likely split into two incompatible AI "regimes." One sphere will be led by the US, prioritizing closed-source, high-compute frontier models; the other by China, prioritizing open-source, embodied intelligence integrated into the global manufacturing supply chain.1  
* **The "Safety Squeeze":** In the absence of a breakthrough in verification technology, the Prisoner’s Dilemma will continue to drive "mutual defection" on safety.4 Corporations will likely "cut corners" to reach AGI first, increasing the risk of catastrophic accidents or rogue AI behavior.17  
* **SaaS Consolidation and Agentic Hegemony:** Traditional SaaS will continue to undergo massive consolidation.23 The market will be dominated by a few "Winner-Take-Most" platforms (e.g., Salesforce, Microsoft, Google) that own the data and the autonomous agents that execute workflows.20  
* **Kinetic AI Escalation:** Current wars will accelerate the normalization of lethal autonomous weapons.27 The first "AI flash war" is a distinct possibility as automated systems reduce the time for human judgment in military escalation.17

### **2\. Strategic Recommendations for Global Actors**

* **For Corporations:** Prioritize "workflow ownership" and "proprietary data moats" rather than thin technology layers.23 Transition from subscription models to consumption-based pricing to capture the value of AI execution.20  
* **For Middle Powers:** Focus on "absorption capacity" and "Sovereign AI" infrastructure.14 Invest in locally-owned data centers and energy reserves to mitigate the volatility of the US-China compute war.7  
* **For Regulators:** Shift from abstract "ethics" to "verifiable safety standards".15 Support the development of hardware-level verification and "red lines" for high-risk domains like biosecurity and nuclear command and control.15

The AI race of 2025 is a "continuous cycle of improvement" that challenges our ethics and redefines what it means to be human.6 While the risks are existential, the potential for AGI to solve the most pressing challenges of humanity—from curing diseases to accelerating the green transition—remains the primary driver of this frantic global contest.2 The ultimate outcome will be determined not just by who has the most chips, but by who can most effectively manage the strategic dilemmas of speed versus caution and competition versus collusion.5
